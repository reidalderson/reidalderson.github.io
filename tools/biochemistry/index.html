Rotational correlation time (τc) from 15N relaxation


One can calculate the rotational correlation time (τc) of a biomolecule by NMR given the static magnetic field strength (B0) and the 15N longitudinal and transverse relaxation rates (R1, R2). For folded proteins, the τc is valuable because it is related to the size of the particle – and, therefore, the molecular mass. Thus, the τc can be used to quickly determine the oligomeric state.


However, there are a couple of caveats. First, the 15N relaxation rates should be averaged value over a group of residues that do not show evidence of extensive motions on the ps-ns or μs-ms timescales. Such residues can be identified from a relatively low value of the heteronuclear NOE (e.g., below 0.65 at 600 MHz) or a higher-than-average R2 rate, respectively. Second, the rotational diffusion of the biomolecue impacts the τc. The calculator below assumes an isotropic rotational diffusion tensor; deviations from this assumption will impact the accuracy of the calculated τc. It is not uncommon for proteins to diffuse with axially symmetric or anisotropic rotational diffusion tensors.
